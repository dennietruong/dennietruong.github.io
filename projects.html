NASA Student Airborne Research Program (2023)

Title: Potential environmental drivers of algal blooms & utility of LANDSAT characterization of the James River

Algal bloom is a rapid increase or accumulation of algae in a body of water caused by excess nutrients, usually from agricultural runoff or sewage effluent. The effects of blooms are devastating and can lead to fish kills, adverse health effects on the community, and wildlife mortality. This project explores environmental factors driving algal blooms and evaluates LANDSAT 8 data for characterizing blooms along the James River. I developed models and used LANDSAT imagery to improve environmental monitoring efforts.

Bigelow Laboratory for Ocean Sciences (2022–2025)

Title: Marine and synthetic glutamatergic neurotoxins in intertidal sea stars

Beginning in 2013, a massive die-off known as sea star wasting impacted billions of sea stars along the west coast of North America. We investigated whether domoic acid, a neurotoxin produced by algae, contributes to their decline by measuring toxin levels in sea stars and observing physiological effects.

  • Measured accumulation of marine and synthetic neurotoxins in sea star tissues
  • Conducted experimental injections to study physiological responses
  • Provided insights into ecological impacts and species resilience

Colby College, Ornithology (2024)

Title: Spatial and Temporal Visualization of Purple Martin

Purple Martins are long-distance migratory birds that breed across North America in the spring and summer before traveling to South America for the winter. Unfortunately, their populations have declined by 24.1% over the past 55 years, amounting to an estimated loss of 2.8 million birds. This decline is driven in part by habitat loss and increased competition from non-native invasive species, including the English House Sparrow and the European Starling.

As aerial insectivores, Purple Martins play an important ecological role by capturing insects in flight. Studies estimate that they consume approximately 262 billion insects each year, many of which are pests affecting both human health and agricultural productivity. This project uses survey data from the Purple Martin Conservation Association to examine spatial patterns in arrival dates over time and to assess how these patterns vary from year to year.

Bigelow Sea Semester Program (2022)

Title: Spatial & temporal trends of zooplankton biomass along the Damariscotta River

This project examines the relationship between chlorophyll, temperature, and zooplankton biomass, a critical area of study because zooplankton are central to ocean ecosystem functioning. Through their daily vertical migrations, they actively transport carbon and nitrogen to the deep sea, contributing to the biological pump and influencing global biogeochemical cycles. Zooplankton populations closely track seasonal and interannual changes in environmental conditions, making them valuable indicators of ecosystem health. By analyzing how chlorophyll and temperature relate to zooplankton biomass, this project leverages zooplankton as a proxy for monitoring changes in local waters. Understanding these relationships can improve our ability to predict ecosystem fluctuations and support sustainable management of ocean resources.

Woodcraft Rangers (2025-PRESENT)

Baseball Dashboards

For this project, I developed interactive dashboards to analyze player performance during the 2025 Spring Baseball season. Data was collected for each player across all eight games of the season, including key statistics such as batting averages, on-base percentages, strikeouts, walks, innings pitched, earned runs, and more. These metrics were analyzed to identify individual strengths and areas for improvement, as well as to support coaching decisions on game strategies and player development. The dashboards allow for interactive exploration of the data, making it easier to visualize trends, compare player performance over time, and make data-driven recommendations for training and in-game tactics.
